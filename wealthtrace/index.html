WealthTrace

Making a sophisticated retirement-planning engine legible enough to convert

Retirement-planning software for high-net-worth U.S. investors: a modeling engine with a strong reputation, wrapped in a surface that was losing the users the engine had already won. I own the redesign of the entire web app.

Context

An institutional-grade engine sold to consumers

WealthTrace serves high-net-worth U.S. investors — $2–10M investable, age 50–65, highly financially literate. Its edge is modeling depth: a Monte Carlo engine that computes weighted return and volatility down to the individual holding, which puts it closer to advisor tools (RightCapital, eMoney) than to consumer apps (Boldin).

Strong engine, weak surface. I operate as the sole designer with end-to-end ownership of the web app — discovery, concept, design-system foundation, every product flow, and the developer-ready handoff.

The problem

High-intent trials that never converted

Users arrived through high-intent searches — “Roth conversion,” “retirement planning” — yet only about 13 of 131 trials a month became paid. Growth was flat (~5% YoY, mostly word of mouth), and many users dropped off mid-trial.

The working hypothesis: the surface — dated, overwhelming, hard to navigate — was losing users the engine had already won. The goal: make the product clear enough that non-technical, high-intent users reach value and convert.

Diagnosis

Finding what was actually losing users

The team’s initial theory pointed at the tab-based navigation. My heuristic analysis said otherwise: tabs weren’t the problem, and removing them was neither possible nor desirable given the product’s structure.

The real causes were low signal-to-noise — screens carried content users never looked at — and poor semantic grouping — related information wasn’t organized by meaning. So the redesign targeted the cause, not the symptom, and that diagnosis set the direction for every decision that follows.

Key decisions

Six decisions that rebuilt the product

Decision 1

Linked Entities — data integrity by design

On the cash-flow inputs screen, one real-world object required several disconnected records: buy a rental property and you’d add the asset, the mortgage (a liability), the rental income (an inflow), and the future sale (another inflow) — four entries for one thing. The product team’s moderated interviews with real users surfaced the consequences: forgotten entries, and orphaned records left behind after manual deletion.

I owned the design response: a Linked Entities model where an asset owns its dependencies. Creating a property generates the linked liability and income inside it; editing or deleting the parent cascades to everything linked. In hallway tests, users understood the model immediately — and expressed visible relief at no longer cleaning up scattered records by hand.

Why it’s a product decision: this data feeds the Monte Carlo engine. Orphaned records corrupted the very projections the product is known for — Linked Entities is a state-management and data-model decision, not a cosmetic one.
Decision 2

Granular allocation — progressive disclosure under tight constraints

Manually entered accounts support extreme detail for accurate probability modeling: holdings down to individual tickers, allocation by type (e.g. 30% cash / 50% growth stocks / 5% bonds). That’s a deeply nested structure — Account → asset class → tickers → % — that had to fit a tight interface without turning into an endless spreadsheet. I designed a compact UI built on progressive disclosure and inline editing, so users distribute a 100% allocation comfortably. It took heavy iteration to get right.

Decision 3

Monte Carlo — settings vs results, trust by design

The Monte Carlo screen mixed inputs and outputs in the same visual space, so users couldn’t tell which controls they owned versus which numbers the model produced. A support-confirmed failure followed: the simulation re-runs manually, so people changed a setting and read stale results as current. I pulled all settings into a dedicated column, gave results their own primary area, and added a state cue — a subtle animation and indicator that fires when a setting changes, signaling the simulation is stale and must be re-run.

Why it’s a product decision: I deliberately left the model untouched — the engine was sound. For an accuracy-obsessed audience, the job was making a strong engine legible and trustworthy, not redesigning its math.

Decision 4

FlowView — reading cash flow at a glance

The cash-flow distributions screen is a dense year-by-year table of inflows and outflows by category — taxes, sources, destinations. I proposed and designed a flow-style visualization on top of the table, so users can see at a glance where money comes from and where it goes, turning a spreadsheet into something readable in seconds.

Decision 5 · the diagnosis, applied

Fighting information overload

The dashboard showed charts nobody used. The projections screen carried an asset-allocation chart with no semantic relationship to projections — users explicitly said they didn’t understand why it was there. I raised the signal-to-noise ratio by cutting low-value content, then applied semantic grouping to organize what remained by meaning. Both screens became focused, with a clear hierarchy around the inputs and projections that matter.

Decision 6

Onboarding rebuilt around time-to-value

The old onboarding stacked heavy, form-crowded steps before the user saw anything valuable. I rebuilt it: a minimal first step (name, email, password), goal capture, inline financial-planning tips linked to their sources, and optional early account entry that triggers a live projection graph — capital growth updating as the user adds income, property, and expenses, before they ever reach the dashboard. A review step lets users correct their data, and a brief “calculating 100,000 scenarios…” screen makes the depth of the modeling felt. I also removed the in-onboarding two-factor verification that was costing time and goodwill.

Validated: in a comparative test, new users ran both the old and the new onboarding while time-on-task and errors were measured. The new flow won clearly — ~30% faster (exact figure TBC).
Process — AI-assisted product design

Specs did the designing; the tool did the drawing

This project ran on a deliberately novel production process. I built the visual concept, a foundational design system (color/type tokens, base components), and the first dashboard in Figma. Then I moved production into an AI design tool — but the critical input wasn’t pixels, it was specification: before each screen I wrote dense page-level documentation — every field, how it behaves, why the data is collected, the helper copy, the interaction logic.

The tool produced clickable, responsive, near-final UI from those specs; I iterated through stakeholder feedback, then rebuilt approved designs in Figma as the developer source of truth. The effect: wireframing collapsed into a high-fidelity, interactive artifact — and the page specs are the proof of authorship. The product thinking, IA, states, and logic were mine; the tool accelerated execution.

Reflection

The decisions I’d defend hardest are the invisible ones — Linked Entities and the Monte Carlo settings/results split — because they protect the thing users actually pay for: projections they can trust.
